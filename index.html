Reframing the ML/GenAI Pipeline

Why reframe the pipeline? The familiar ‚Äúcollect ‚Üí clean ‚Üí train‚Äù storyline obscures the curatorial and ethical work that shapes model behaviour. A curation-centred view (informed by UK DCC principles) surfaces task context, interoperability, provenance, and stewardship as first-class concerns in both classical ML and GenAI.

  • Context first: task definition and intended use establish inclusion rules, success criteria, and risk posture.
  • Interoperability: transformation/standardisation enable reproducibility and reuse across teams and time.
  • Alignment as data work: labels, pairs, and preference data encode values and semantics.
  • Provenance & licensing: documentation, consent, and versioning are essential to accountability.
  • Ethics ‚Üí evaluation: performance includes safety, fairness, calibration, and cultural fit‚Äînot metrics alone.

Data Curation

Task definition, acquisition, documentation, transformation, alignment, quality control.

DCC principlesContext & Standards

Explanation: Active stewardship of data across its lifecycle ‚Äî making it meaningful, standardised, interoperable, and reusable. It includes the sub-processes below.

Task Definition

Goals, scope, ethics.

Define goals, stakeholders, risks, and success criteria. Establish inclusion/exclusion rules and anticipate fairness and compliance needs.

Data Acquisition

Source, sample, license, representativeness.

Source and sample data to match the task and population. Ensure licensing, consent, and representativeness; plan balance (classical ML) or coverage/diversity (GenAI).

Documentation

Provenance and metadata.

Record origins, transformations, and intended uses (e.g. Datasheets for Datasets, Model Cards). Track lineage, retention, and access controls for accountability and reuse.

Transformation

Schemas, normalisation, tokenisation.

Define schemas and reproducible pipelines: cleaning, scaling, tokenisation, and mapping for interoperability and reproducibility.

Alignment

Labels, pairs, preference data.

Create labels, pairs, and preference data; validate and resolve conflicts; document uncertainty. For multimodal/GenAI, include caption and text‚Äìimage alignment checks.

Quality Control

Deduplication, bias audits, drift, preservation.

Deduplicate and filter; audit bias and toxicity; monitor drift; version dataset releases; and design archival and access strategies to keep data usable over time.

Model Development

Pre-training, fine-tuning, and interfaces.

Explanation: Build and adapt models using curated data. Classical ML: supervised training and feature engineering. GenAI: self-supervised pre-training, instruction fine-tuning, and safety alignment.

  • Training types ‚Äì supervised, self-supervised, contrastive.
  • Adaptation ‚Äì LoRA/adapters, instruction tuning, domain specialisation.
  • Interfaces ‚Äì features (classic) vs prompts/tools (GenAI).

Evaluation

Metrics and human review for quality and robustness.

Explanation: Combine quantitative metrics with human-centred evaluation. Benchmarks reveal progress but must be paired with qualitative review and subgroup analysis.

  • Metrics ‚Äì accuracy/F1, BLEU/FID, calibration and robustness.
  • Human evaluation ‚Äì red-teaming, UX quality, expert review.
  • Fairness ‚Äì sub-group performance and harm analysis.

Deployment

Serving, performance, cost, privacy, compliance.

Explanation: Serve models reliably and responsibly while managing latency, throughput, cost, security, and compliance.

  • Roll-out ‚Äì canary/shadow, A/B testing, versioning.
  • Optimisation ‚Äì batching, caching, quantisation.
  • Privacy & access ‚Äì data boundaries and audit logs.

Monitoring

Telemetry, drift, feedback loops, governance.

Explanation: Continuous oversight of performance, safety, and societal impact. Manage drift, incidents, and retraining cycles.

  • Observability ‚Äì telemetry, alerts, incident response.
  • Drift ‚Äì detect data or concept drift and regressions.
  • Feedback ‚Äì user signals and lifecycle governance.

Why each stage of the pipeline needs attention

Why consider ethical risks at every stage? Ethical issues arise at every stage, from how data are defined and collected to how models are evaluated and monitored.

  • Each stage has unique actors and decisions: curators, developers, evaluators, and deployers all hold different responsibilities and leverage points.
  • Bias and harm compound over time: early-stage imbalances in representation or process can amplify through model training and fine-tuning.
  • Context shapes fairness: cultural, linguistic, and domain-specific contexts alter what counts as ethical, safe, or fair performance.
  • Transparency builds trust: documenting ethical considerations across the pipeline supports accountability and public confidence.
  • Legal and societal expectations matter: equality, privacy, and online safety laws link directly to data handling and AI behaviour.
  • Ethics is continuous, not static: post-deployment feedback and monitoring must inform retraining, auditing, and policy updates.

PHAWM Project Overview and Update

Overview, deliverables, use cases, and how to get involved.

What is Participatory AI Auditing?

Bring non-technical stakeholders into harm/fairness auditing.
  • Broader expertise: health practitioners, frontline workers, end users, and the public co-audit AI systems.
  • Predictive + Generative: applicable to classification/forecasting as well as LLMs and multimodal models.
  • Context-aware: audits are grounded in local tasks, norms, cultures, and risk tolerances.
  • Transparent process: documented measures, decisions, and pass/fail criteria.

What PHAWM Delivers

Workbench, methodology, training, and certification exploration.
  • Auditing Workbench: guided workflows so non-experts can run harm/fairness assessments end-to-end, individually or collectively.
  • Methodology: define scope, stakeholders, context-specific measures, evidence, and reporting.
  • Training Pathways: materials for public sector, industry, and civil society auditors.
  • Exploring Certification: self-certification based on criteria and evidence to support external assurance.

Who It‚Äôs For

Roles and communities that can participate.
  • Public & third sector: policy teams, regulators, trusts, NGOs.
  • Domain experts: clinicians, educators, librarians, heritage curators, moderators.
  • Product teams: PMs, UX researchers, safety/red-teamers.
  • Communities & end users: affected groups defining meaningful pass/fail criteria.

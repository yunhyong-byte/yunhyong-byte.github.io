1) Digital Preservation: Core Principles — ensure long-term access, authenticity, usability

Sustainability & Continuity. Preserve born-digital (including oral histories recorded digitally, videos, social media) and digitised heritage (photos, letters, posters) in formats that remain accessible over time.

Authenticity & Integrity. Maintain trustworthy, well-documented materials with transparent versioning rather than insisting they remain unaltered; include robust metadata, provenance and context—capturing lives, cultural practice, activism and contributions.

Access & Ethics. Balance openness with cultural sensitivity, data protection and community rights to narrate their own stories.

2) Digital Curation: Process & Practice — life cycle management from creation to reuse

Appraisal & Selection. Choose materials representing lived experience and contributions of Scottish BME communities—such as oral history interviews, photographic archives, community newsletters/zines, and records of activism, art and cultural festivals.

Community Collaboration. Co-curate with community members (as co-researchers or co-archivists) to ensure authenticity, relevance and self-representation; participatory approaches mitigate institutional bias.

“If you don’t turn your life into a story, you just become a part of someone else’s story.” — Terry Pratchett, The Amazing Maurice and His Educated Rodents

Documentation & Description (Metadata). Create rich, inclusive metadata acknowledging cultural identity/self-description, historical context/community narratives, and sensitivity labels (e.g., sacred or personal). Use standards such as Dublin Core, Metadata for Indigenous Collections and UKAD guidelines as adaptable references.

Digital Stewardship & Access Design. Build repositories that prioritise community-managed archives and platforms, in partnership with university or national repositories, with multilingual metadata, strong searchability and WCAG-compliant access; use narrative-driven interfaces that connect “then” and “now”.

3) Selection Criteria for Preservation — guide choices in heritage projects
  • Representation: Include under-represented BME voices in Scottish history.
  • Uniqueness: Prioritise materials likely to be lost or overlooked.
  • Relevance: Capture change and continuity in BME experiences.
  • Quality & Context: Ensure sufficient metadata, provenance and narrative framing to preserve meaning.
“There is no greater agony than bearing an untold story inside you.” — Maya Angelou, I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings
4) Ethical & Decolonial Dimensions — governance, consent and context
  • Community Ownership, Governance, Control & Consent: Contributors retain agency over their stories and data.
  • Contextualisation: Avoid tokenism by situating materials within migration, racism, resilience and creativity narratives.
  • Reflexivity: Institutions practice critical self-awareness and acknowledge positionality when preserving and interpreting histories that have been systemically excluded.
5) Then & Now Approach — temporal & narrative curation strategy
  • Then — Archival Recovery: Rediscover overlooked historical materials from communities in Scotland.
  • Now — Digital Storytelling: Preserve ongoing community work, artistic production and oral traditions in contemporary digital formats.
  • Connection: Build interpretive frameworks tracing continuity and transformation across time.
“People think that stories are shaped by people. In fact, it’s the other way around.” — Terry Pratchett, Witches Abroad
